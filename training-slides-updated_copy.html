HIPAA & Patient Privacy: The Foundation

What is PHI (Protected Health Information)?

Any information about health status, healthcare provision, or payment that can identify a patient. This includes names, addresses, dates, medical records, insurance information, photos, and even conversation about patients.

Legal Obligations & Penalties

  • Individual fines: Up to $50,000 per violation
  • Practice liability: Up to $2.1 million per year
  • Criminal charges: Possible jail time for intentional breaches
  • Reputation damage: Loss of patient trust and business

Why It Matters

Patients trust us with their most personal information. One breach can destroy years of trust, harm our patients, and close our practice. Every team member is responsible for protecting patient privacy.

Phishing & Email Safety: Spotting Threats

What is Phishing?

Phishing emails trick you into clicking malicious links, downloading malware, or revealing sensitive information. They often impersonate trusted sources like insurance companies, patients, or EMR vendors.

90% of healthcare breaches start with a phishing email.

üö© Red Flags: How to Spot Phishing

Urgent language

"Verify your account NOW or it will be suspended!"

Generic greetings

"Dear User" instead of your name

Suspicious sender

bluecross@insuranceverify[.]com (not official domain)

Unexpected attachments

Invoice.pdf from unknown sender

Spelling/grammar errors

"Your patient informations needs verify"

Requests for credentials

Asking for password or login information

PT Practice-Specific Phishing Examples

üìß Fake appointment requests: "Please click here to confirm John Smith's therapy appointment"

üìß Insurance verification scams: "Action Required: Verify Medicare credentials to continue receiving payments"

üìß EMR vendor impersonation: "Your WebPT account needs immediate security update - login here"

üìß Patient impersonation: "Can you email me my medical records? I'm traveling and need them urgently"

Phishing & Email Safety: What to Do

‚úì Safe Email Practices

  • ‚Ä¢ Hover before clicking: Hover your mouse over links to preview the actual URL before clicking
  • ‚Ä¢ Verify the sender: Check the full email address, not just the display name
  • ‚Ä¢ Call to confirm: For ANY unusual request involving patient data or payments, call the person using a number you already have (not one in the email)
  • ‚Ä¢ Type URLs manually: Go directly to websites by typing the address instead of clicking email links
  • ‚Ä¢ Check for HTTPS: Look for the padlock icon in your browser when entering sensitive information
  • ‚Ä¢ Use secure portals: Share patient information through your EMR's secure messaging, never regular email

‚ö†Ô∏è If You Click Something Suspicious

Step 1: Don't panic, but act quickly

Step 2: Disconnect from the internet - Unplug ethernet or turn off Wi-Fi

Step 3: Report immediately - Contact your IT person/manager right away (even if after hours)

Step 4: Don't try to fix it yourself - Don't delete emails or close windows; leave everything as-is

Step 5: Change passwords - Once IT gives the all-clear, change your passwords from a different device

Remember: Quick reporting limits damage. You won't be blamed for an honest mistake, but hiding it puts everyone at risk.

Password Security: Your First Line of Defense

Why Passwords Matter

Passwords are the keys to patient records, billing systems, and everything in your practice.

81% of healthcare breaches involve weak or stolen passwords.

A single compromised password can expose thousands of patient records.

‚úì Creating Strong Passwords

Use the "passphrase" method: String together 4+ random words with numbers/symbols

‚ùå Bad: Therapy2024 or Password123!
‚úÖ Good: Purple$Elephant47!Mountain
‚úÖ Good: Coffee-Train-82-Garden
‚úÖ Good: Monday&Coffee#Always%First

  • ‚Ä¢ Minimum 12 characters (longer is better)
  • ‚Ä¢ Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  • ‚Ä¢ Avoid personal info (birthdays, names, practice name)
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't use common words or patterns (abc123, qwerty)
  • ‚Ä¢ Every system needs a different password - EMR, email, billing, etc.

üîë Password Managers: Your Secret Weapon

A password manager securely stores all your passwords so you only need to remember ONE master password.

Popular options: 1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane

Benefits: Generates strong passwords automatically, fills them in for you, works across all devices, alerts you to breached passwords

üí° Tip: Many practices provide a team password manager. Ask your manager if one is available!

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Your Best Defense

What is Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?

MFA requires two or more verification methods to access an account. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without the second factor.

MFA blocks 99.9% of automated account attacks.

The Three Authentication Factors

Something You KNOW

Password, PIN, security questions

Something You HAVE

Phone, security key, authentication app

Something You ARE

Fingerprint, face scan, voice

MFA combines at least two of these factors. Example: Password (know) + code from your phone (have)

Common MFA Methods (Ranked by Security)

‚úÖ MOST SECURE: Passkeys & Hardware Keys

Physical security keys (like YubiKey) or device-based passkeys. Nearly impossible to phish or intercept.

‚úÖ VERY SECURE: Authenticator Apps

Apps like Microsoft Authenticator, Google Authenticator, Duo. Generate time-based codes on your phone.

‚úì SECURE: Push Notifications

App sends approval request to your phone. Convenient but can be accidentally approved.

‚ö†Ô∏è LESS SECURE: SMS Text Codes

6-digit code sent via text. Better than nothing, but vulnerable to SIM swapping attacks.

‚úó AVOID: Email Codes

If your email is compromised, the attacker gets the code. Not recommended for critical accounts.

Where to Enable MFA in Your Practice

Enable MFA on ALL of these accounts:

  • ‚Ä¢ Email accounts (Microsoft 365, Gmail) - your #1 priority
  • ‚Ä¢ EMR/Practice Management System (WebPT, TherapyNotes, etc.)
  • ‚Ä¢ Billing and financial systems
  • ‚Ä¢ Password manager
  • ‚Ä¢ Cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive)
  • ‚Ä¢ Banking and payment accounts
  • ‚Ä¢ Any system with patient data

Ask your IT person or manager to help you set up MFA if you're not sure how. It takes 5-10 minutes and could save your practice.

Passkeys & Advanced MFA: The Future of Security

What are Passkeys?

Passkeys are a newer, more secure alternative to passwords. They use your device (phone, laptop, security key) to prove it's really you. No password to remember, nothing to type, and nearly impossible to phish. Think of it like using your fingerprint or face to unlock your phone, but for websites and apps.

‚úÖ Why Passkeys are Better Than Passwords

‚úÖ Phishing-Proof

Passkeys only work on the real website. Even if you visit a fake site, the passkey won't unlock.

‚úÖ No Password to Steal

There's no password stored anywhere that hackers can steal from a database breach.

‚úÖ Faster Login

Just use Face ID, fingerprint, or PIN on your device. No typing long passwords.

‚úÖ Automatically Secure

You can't create a "weak passkey" - they're all cryptographically strong by default.

How to Set Up & Use Passkeys

Setting up a passkey (typical process):

Step 1: Go to the website's security settings (e.g., Google, Microsoft, your EMR)

Step 2: Look for "Passkeys," "Security Keys," or "Add Sign-in Method"

Step 3: Click "Create a passkey" or "Add passkey"

Step 4: Choose your device (this phone, this computer, or security key)

Step 5: Verify with Face ID, fingerprint, or device PIN

Step 6: Done! Next time you sign in, use the same biometric or PIN instead of a password

Using your passkey: When you log in, click "Sign in with passkey" instead of entering a password. Your device will prompt for Face ID, fingerprint, or PIN - that's it!

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Passkey Considerations

Things to know:

  • ‚Ä¢ Backup your passkeys: Most systems let you save passkeys to iCloud, Google Password Manager, or your password manager. Enable this so you don't lose access if your device breaks.
  • ‚Ä¢ Multiple devices: You can create passkeys on multiple devices (work computer, phone, tablet) for the same account.
  • ‚Ä¢ Keep a backup method: Don't delete your password or other MFA methods until you're confident passkeys are working. Keep alternatives as backup.
  • ‚Ä¢ Not everywhere yet: Not all systems support passkeys. Your EMR, email, and banking might not offer them yet - use strong MFA methods in the meantime.
  • ‚Ä¢ Ask for help: If you're confused about setting up passkeys, ask your IT person or manager for assistance.

Quick Reference: MFA & Passkey Best Practices

  • ‚òë Enable MFA on every critical account (email, EMR, banking, etc.)
  • ‚òë Use authenticator apps or passkeys when available (better than SMS)
  • ‚òë Keep backup authentication methods in case primary device is lost
  • ‚òë Save recovery codes in a secure location (password manager or locked drawer)
  • ‚òë Enable passkeys on accounts that support them for maximum security
  • ‚òë Never share MFA codes or approve login requests you didn't initiate
  • ‚òë Test your MFA and passkeys regularly to ensure they work

Bottom line: MFA and passkeys are your strongest defense against account takeovers. Enable them today!

Physical Security: Protecting Your Space

Why Physical Security Matters

Digital security means nothing if someone can walk in and grab patient files, see information on screens, or access unlocked computers. Physical breaches are easier than cyber attacks and just as damaging.

üö™ Secure Access: Doors, Keys & Visitors

  • ‚Ä¢ Medical records storage rooms
  • ‚Ä¢ Server rooms or areas with computer equipment
  • ‚Ä¢ Staff-only areas when unattended
  • ‚Ä¢ Filing cabinets containing PHI

Visitor management:

  • ‚Ä¢ All visitors (including vendors, repair people) must sign in
  • ‚Ä¢ Non-patients should be escorted in staff areas
  • ‚Ä¢ Visitors should wear visible badges
  • ‚Ä¢ If you see someone unfamiliar in a restricted area, politely ask if they need help

üí° Tailgating prevention: Don't hold doors open for people you don't know, even if they're carrying supplies or wearing uniforms. Ask them to check in at the front desk.

üëÄ Monitor Positioning & Visual Privacy

Position computer monitors away from:

  • ‚Ä¢ Waiting room views
  • ‚Ä¢ Windows visible from outside
  • ‚Ä¢ Patient treatment areas where others can see
  • ‚Ä¢ Common walkways and high-traffic areas

Consider using privacy screens (filters that make screens only visible from directly in front) for workstations in public areas.

Mobile Device & Remote Access Security

Mobile Devices Are High Risk

Phones and tablets are easily lost, stolen, or compromised. 29% of healthcare breaches involve mobile devices. Yet we rely on them for work. This makes securing them critical.

üì± Securing Work Phones & Tablets

Required security measures:

  • ‚Ä¢ Strong passcode/PIN: Minimum 6 digits, not 1234 or repeated numbers
  • ‚Ä¢ Biometric lock: Enable Face ID or fingerprint when available
  • ‚Ä¢ Auto-lock timeout: Set to 1-2 minutes maximum
  • ‚Ä¢ Encryption: Enable full device encryption (usually on by default for newer devices)
  • ‚Ä¢ Keep updated: Install OS and app updates promptly
  • ‚Ä¢ Remote wipe capability: Ensure IT can remotely erase the device if lost

üîí Never disable security features (like auto-lock) because they're "annoying" - they exist to protect patient data.

‚ö†Ô∏è Public Wi-Fi Dangers

Never access patient data on public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, airports, hotels) without a VPN.

Why it's dangerous: Hackers on the same network can intercept your connection and steal passwords or see the data you're accessing.

Safe alternatives:

  • ‚Ä¢ Use your phone's cellular data (4G/5G) instead
  • ‚Ä¢ Use a mobile hotspot from your phone
  • ‚Ä¢ If you must use public Wi-Fi, use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) provided by your practice
  • ‚Ä¢ Wait until you're on a secure network

Mobile Device & Remote Access: Best Practices

üö® Lost or Stolen Device Protocol

If a work device is lost or stolen, every minute counts:

Step 1: Report immediately to IT/manager (within 1 hour if possible)
Step 2: Don't wait to "look for it" - report first, search later
Step 3: IT will remotely lock or wipe the device
Step 4: Change passwords for all work systems from another device
Step 5: Document what patient data might have been on the device

Delayed reporting turns a manageable incident into a major breach requiring patient notification and potential fines.

üíº Personal Device Use (BYOD)

  • ‚Ä¢ Only use approved apps (usually secure messaging within your EMR)
  • ‚Ä¢ Never text patient information via regular SMS
  • ‚Ä¢ Never take photos of patient records or screens with personal phone
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't download patient data to personal devices
  • ‚Ä¢ Apply same security measures: strong passcode, auto-lock, updates
  • ‚Ä¢ Understand IT may require ability to wipe work data from your device

Best practice: Keep work and personal completely separate. Use work devices for patient data whenever possible.

Social Engineering: The Human Hack

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is manipulation that tricks people into breaking security rules. Attackers exploit trust, authority, and helpfulness to get past technical security. It's easier to trick a person than hack a system.

üìû Phone-Based Attacks

Common phone scams targeting PT offices:

Scenario 1: Patient Impersonation

"Hi, this is John Smith. I'm traveling and need you to email my therapy records to my personal email right away."

Red flag: Urgent requests to send PHI to unusual places

Scenario 2: Insurance Representative

"I'm calling from Medicare to verify your billing credentials. I need your login and password to update your account."

Red flag: Legitimate companies never ask for passwords over the phone

Scenario 3: Doctor Impersonation

"This is Dr. Rodriguez calling. I need Sarah Johnson's records immediately for an emergency consultation."

Red flag: Pressure tactics and unfamiliar requests from "authority figures"

üé≠ In-Person & Vendor Impersonation

Watch for these tactics:

  • ‚Ä¢ "IT support" calling: "We detected a problem and need remote access to your computer"
  • ‚Ä¢ Fake delivery person: Needs to verify information before dropping off a package
  • ‚Ä¢ Surveyor or inspector: Claims they need to review files as part of an audit
  • ‚Ä¢ EMR vendor representative: "We're updating your system and need admin access"

üí° Verification rule: When in doubt, hang up and call back using a number you know is legitimate. Don't use contact info provided by the caller.

Social Engineering: How to Respond

‚úì Defense Strategies

Follow these protocols to protect patient information:

  • ‚Ä¢ Verify identity independently: Call back using known phone numbers, don't rely on caller ID
  • ‚Ä¢ Never share credentials: No legitimate vendor or IT person will ask for your password
  • ‚Ä¢ Question urgency: Pressure and time limits are manipulation tactics
  • ‚Ä¢ Use established channels: Patient record requests should go through proper procedures
  • ‚Ä¢ Trust your instincts: If something feels off, it probably is
  • ‚Ä¢ Confirm with supervisor: When unsure, escalate to your manager before sharing information

üõ°Ô∏è Response Scripts You Can Use

It's okay to say NO. Here are polite but firm responses:

For patient information requests:

"I need to verify your identity first. Can you provide the patient's date of birth and the last four digits of their Social Security number? I'll also need to call you back at the number we have on file."

For credential requests:

"Our policy prohibits sharing login credentials over the phone. Please contact our IT department through our official support channel."

For pressure tactics:

"I understand this seems urgent, but I need to follow our security procedures. Let me connect you with my supervisor."

For vendor access requests:

"I'll need to verify this request through our service agreement. Please send an email to our practice administrator."

Practice Scenario: Test Yourself!

You receive a call:

"Hi, this is Dave from TechSupport Solutions. Your EMR vendor hired us to upgrade your security. I need you to download this remote access software so I can update your system. Your practice owner approved this yesterday. Can you click this link I'm texting you?"

Red flags: Unfamiliar vendor, urgency, remote access request, claim of approval, unsolicited link
Response: "I'm not authorized to install software. Please contact our practice administrator at [official email]. I'll let them know you called." Then report the call to your supervisor immediately.

Software Updates & Patch Management: Why Updates Matter

Outdated Software = Open Door for Attackers

73% of successful cyberattacks exploit known vulnerabilities that already have patches available. When you delay updates, you're leaving the door unlocked even though you have the key to lock it. Attackers scan for outdated systems because they're easy targets.

üö® Real-World Impact

WannaCry Ransomware (2017)

Infected 300,000+ computers worldwide, including healthcare systems. The patch was available for 2 months before the attack. Organizations that delayed updates paid the price with ransomware, downtime, and data loss.

Equifax Breach (2017)

147 million people's data stolen. The vulnerability had a patch available for 2 months before the breach. Cost: $575 million in settlements.

Common pattern: Patches are available, but organizations delay installation. Attackers exploit the delay.

üîß What Software Needs Updates?

Everything that connects to the internet or handles patient data:

Operating Systems

Windows, macOS, iOS, Android

Applications

Microsoft Office, Adobe, Zoom, browsers

Healthcare Software

EMR, practice management, billing systems

Security Tools

Antivirus, firewalls, VPNs

Web Browsers

Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Safari

Mobile Apps

Work apps on phones and tablets

‚ö° Types of Updates

üî¥ Security Patches (CRITICAL - Install ASAP)

Fix security vulnerabilities. These are the most important and should be installed within days of release.

üü° Bug Fixes (Install Soon)

Fix software errors and stability issues. Install within 1-2 weeks.

üü¢ Feature Updates (Install When Convenient)

Add new features or improve performance. Can wait longer but should still be installed regularly.

Software Updates & Patch Management: Best Practices

‚úì Update Best Practices

  • ‚Ä¢ Enable automatic updates whenever possible (Windows Update, macOS Software Update, phone updates)
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't click "Remind Me Later" repeatedly - install updates promptly when notified

  • ‚Ä¢ Restart when prompted - Many updates require a restart to take effect; don't delay this
  • ‚Ä¢ Check manually weekly - Even with auto-updates, manually check Windows Update, App Store, etc. weekly
  • ‚Ä¢ Keep mobile devices updated - Phones and tablets need updates too
  • ‚Ä¢ Update browsers regularly - Chrome, Edge, Firefox update automatically; allow them to restart

Data Backup & Recovery: Your Safety Net

Without Backups, You're One Click Away from Disaster

60% of small businesses that lose their data close within 6 months. Ransomware, hardware failure, human error, theft, or natural disasters can destroy your data instantly. Backups are your only way to recover and continue operating. Without them, you're gambling with your entire practice.

üö® Data Loss Scenarios

Ransomware Attack

Attackers encrypt all your patient records and demand $50,000 to unlock them. Without backups, you either pay or lose everything. With backups, you restore and keep operating.

Hardware Failure

Server hard drive fails. Years of patient records, billing data, and schedules are gone instantly. Recovery companies charge $3,000-$15,000 with no guarantee of success.

Human Error

Employee accidentally deletes critical files or overwrites important data. Without backups, it's permanently lost.

Theft or Natural Disaster

Fire, flood, or theft destroys your office equipment. All on-site data is gone unless you have offsite backups.

üì¶ The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

This is the gold standard for data protection:

3

Three copies of your data

The original plus two backups. If one backup fails, you have another.

2

Two different storage types

Don't rely on one technology. Use combinations like: local hard drive + cloud, or external drive + cloud.

1

One copy offsite

At least one backup must be offsite (cloud or physically different location). Protects against fire, flood, or theft destroying everything on-site.

Example for a PT practice: Original data on server ‚Üí Backup 1 on local NAS ‚Üí Backup 2 in cloud (Carbonite, Backblaze, or AWS)

üíæ What Needs to Be Backed Up?

Everything critical to your business operations:

Patient Records

EMR data, medical histories, treatment notes

Financial Data

Billing records, invoices, payment history

Scheduling Data

Appointments, staff schedules, calendars

Business Documents

Contracts, policies, insurance documents

Email Data

Important correspondence, attachments

System Configurations

Software settings, user accounts, templates

Data Backup & Recovery: Making It Work

‚öôÔ∏è Backup Best Practices

Automate Everything

Manual backups will be forgotten. Set up automatic daily backups that run overnight. Verify they're working weekly.

Backup Frequency

Critical data (patient records): Daily or continuous
Important data (documents): Daily
System images: Weekly or monthly

Retention Policy

Keep multiple versions: 30 daily backups, 12 monthly backups, 7 yearly backups. This protects against corruption discovered weeks later.

Encryption

All backups containing PHI must be encrypted. Most cloud backup services do this automatically.

Test Restores Regularly

Test restoring files quarterly. An untested backup is just hope. Verify you can actually recover your data before you need it.

‚òÅÔ∏è Cloud Backup vs Local Backup

Cloud Backup ‚úÖ

Pros:

  • ‚Ä¢ Automatic and hands-off
  • ‚Ä¢ Protected from local disasters
  • ‚Ä¢ Accessible from anywhere
  • ‚Ä¢ Provider manages hardware

Cons:

  • ‚Ä¢ Requires internet connection
  • ‚Ä¢ Monthly subscription cost
  • ‚Ä¢ Initial backup can be slow

Pros:

  • ‚Ä¢ Fast backups and restores
  • ‚Ä¢ No internet required
  • ‚Ä¢ One-time hardware cost
  • ‚Ä¢ Complete control

Cons:

  • ‚Ä¢ Vulnerable to local disasters
  • ‚Ä¢ Hardware can fail
  • ‚Ä¢ Requires manual management

Popular options: External hard drives, NAS (Network Attached Storage)

Best approach: Use BOTH! Local backup for fast recovery from minor issues + cloud backup for disaster recovery.

üîí Protecting Backups from Ransomware

Modern ransomware targets backups. If attackers can encrypt your backups along with your primary data, you have no recovery option. Protect backups with these strategies:

  • ‚Ä¢ Immutable backups: Use backup solutions that create "immutable" copies that cannot be deleted or encrypted for a set period (30-90 days)
  • ‚Ä¢ Offline backups: Keep at least one backup physically disconnected from the network
  • ‚Ä¢ Air-gapped backups: Periodically create backups on external drives that are removed and stored offsite
  • ‚Ä¢ Separate credentials: Backup system should have different admin credentials than primary systems
  • ‚Ä¢ Monitor backup integrity: Receive alerts if backups fail or are tampered with

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Backup Mistakes

  • ‚Ä¢ Assuming IT handles it: Verify with IT/manager that backups are configured and working
  • ‚Ä¢ Never testing restores: You don't have a backup unless you've proven you can restore from it
  • ‚Ä¢ Only backing up to one location: One backup = no redundancy. Always have multiple copies
  • ‚Ä¢ Keeping backups on-site only: Fire/flood/theft destroys everything. Must have offsite copy
  • ‚Ä¢ Manual backups only: Humans forget. Automation is essential
  • ‚Ä¢ Not encrypting backups: Unencrypted backups of PHI violate HIPAA
  • ‚Ä¢ Ignoring backup alerts: If backups are failing, data is at risk. Address issues immediately

üìã Your Role in Data Protection

As an employee, you should:

  • ‚òê Know where your practice backs up data and how often
  • ‚òê Understand the recovery time if systems go down
  • ‚òê Report immediately if you suspect data loss or corruption
  • ‚òê Save critical work to backed-up locations (not just local desktop)
  • ‚òê Don't delete files from backup systems
  • ‚òê Alert management if backup notifications/alerts appear

Ask your manager: "How often do we back up? Where are backups stored? When was the last restore test?" These questions could save your practice.

Email Security: Technical Protections

Email: The #1 Attack Vector

91% of cyberattacks start with a phishing email. Email is how attackers deliver malware, steal credentials, and trick employees. Beyond training staff to spot phishing, your email system needs technical security controls that block threats before they reach inboxes. This slide covers the behind-the-scenes protections.

üõ°Ô∏è Email Authentication Protocols

These three protocols prove email authenticity and prevent spoofing (fake sender addresses):

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

Verifies that email comes from an authorized server for your domain. Prevents attackers from sending fake emails that appear to be from your practice.

What it does: "Only these servers can send email as @yourpractice.com"

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

Adds a digital signature to outgoing emails. Receiving servers verify the signature to confirm the email hasn't been tampered with.

What it does: "This email is genuinely from us and hasn't been modified"

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance)

Tells receiving servers what to do if SPF or DKIM checks fail. Also sends you reports about who's sending email using your domain name.

What it does: "If email fails authentication, quarantine or reject it, and send me reports"

Why these matter: Without SPF/DKIM/DMARC, attackers can easily send emails that appear to be from your practice, tricking patients and partners. These protocols make spoofing nearly impossible.

üîç Spam & Phishing Filters

Multi-layered filtering catches threats before they reach your inbox:

Content Filtering

Scans email content for spam indicators, malicious links, dangerous attachments, and suspicious patterns

Reputation-Based Filtering

Blocks emails from known malicious IP addresses and domains using real-time blacklists

Attachment Scanning

Automatically scans attachments for malware before delivery. Blocks dangerous file types (.exe, .scr, macros)

Link Protection

Rewrites URLs to scan them before you click. Blocks access to known phishing and malware sites

Sandboxing

Opens suspicious attachments in an isolated environment to detect hidden malware before delivery

üí° Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace include many of these protections, but may need configuration. Ask IT if advanced threat protection is enabled.

üîê Email Encryption for PHI

Regular email is like a postcard - anyone can read it. Sending patient information via regular email violates HIPAA. Use these encryption methods:

TLS (Transport Layer Security)

Encrypts email in transit between mail servers. Protects against interception but doesn't protect at rest. Should be enabled by default.

Secure Email Portals

Best for HIPAA: Sends notification email with a link to a secure portal where recipient logs in to read the message. Patient data never travels via regular email.

Examples: Paubox, Hushmail, LuxSci, Microsoft 365 Message Encryption

S/MIME or PGP Encryption

End-to-end encryption using digital certificates. Both sender and recipient need certificates/keys. Complex to set up but very secure.

Your practice's policy: Check with your manager which encryption method your practice uses. Never send PHI via regular, unencrypted email.

Access Control & Least Privilege: Who Gets What

The Principle of Least Privilege

Every person should have the minimum access necessary to do their job - nothing more. This limits damage if an account is compromised, reduces insider threats, and ensures HIPAA compliance. If you don't need access to something for your daily work, you shouldn't have access to it.

üö® Why Access Control Matters

Scenario: Excessive Access = Excessive Risk

A front desk employee has admin access "just in case." Their email gets phished, giving attackers full access to all systems, patient records, and financial data. If they only had access to scheduling, the breach would be limited to appointment data.

Scenario: Insider Threat

An employee with broad access inappropriately views celebrity patient records or their ex-partner's medical history. Role-based access would have prevented this HIPAA violation.

The risk equation: More access = more damage if compromised. Least privilege minimizes blast radius.

üîë Types of Access Control

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

Access is granted based on job role, not individual person. All front desk staff get the same permissions. All therapists get the same permissions.

Example roles in a PT practice:

‚Ä¢ Front Desk: Scheduling, check-in/out, patient demographics

‚Ä¢ Physical Therapist: Patient records, treatment notes, documentation

‚Ä¢ Billing Staff: Insurance info, payments, claims

‚Ä¢ Practice Manager: All systems plus admin functions

Need-to-Know Access

Even within a role, access may be further limited. Example: Therapists should only access records of their own patients, not all patients in the system.

Administrative vs Standard Access

Admin accounts can change settings, add/remove users, access audit logs. Most staff should have standard (non-admin) access. Admin access should be limited to 1-2 people.

Time-Limited Access

Temporary access for specific situations (covering for a coworker, special project). Access expires automatically after a set period.

üë• Implementing Access Control in Your Practice

How to do access control right:

1. Define Roles Based on Job Functions

List what each role actually needs to do their job. Don't give "just in case" access.

2. Review Access Regularly

Quarterly audit: Who has access to what? Remove access for departed employees immediately. Adjust when roles change.

3. Use System-Level Controls

Configure EMR, file shares, and practice management systems to enforce role-based permissions. Don't rely on "honor system."

4. Document Everything

HIPAA requires documentation of who has access to what and why. Keep access control lists updated.

5. Separate Duties

The person who enters billing shouldn't also approve payments. The person who schedules appointments shouldn't have admin access.

Access Control & Least Privilege: Your Responsibilities

‚úì Best Practices for Access Control

  • ‚Ä¢ Use your own account only: Never log in with someone else's credentials or share your login
  • ‚Ä¢ Access only what you need: Don't browse patient records out of curiosity. Access only records necessary for your work
  • ‚Ä¢ Log out when done: Don't stay logged into systems you're not actively using
  • ‚Ä¢ Request access properly: If you need access to something new, submit a formal request through your manager
  • ‚Ä¢ Report access issues: If you have access to things you shouldn't, or can't access things you need, report it
  • ‚Ä¢ Understand your permissions: Know what you're allowed to access and what's off-limits
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't escalate your own privileges: Never try to bypass access controls or gain unauthorized access

üîç Audit Trails & Monitoring

Your access is logged and monitored. This isn't about distrust - it's about security and compliance.

What Gets Logged

  • ‚Ä¢ Who accessed what patient records and when
  • ‚Ä¢ Login attempts (successful and failed)
  • ‚Ä¢ Changes to patient data, schedules, or billing
  • ‚Ä¢ Administrative actions (adding users, changing permissions)
  • ‚Ä¢ File access on network shares
  • ‚Ä¢ System changes and configurations

Why This Matters

  • ‚Ä¢ HIPAA compliance: Required by law to track who accesses PHI
  • ‚Ä¢ Detect breaches: Unusual access patterns may indicate compromised account
  • ‚Ä¢ Investigate incidents: If there's a problem, logs show what happened
  • ‚Ä¢ Accountability: Ensures everyone follows rules and policies

üí° Bottom line: If you access patient records, assume it's logged. Only access records you have a legitimate need to see for your job.

‚úó Access Control Violations

These actions violate HIPAA and practice policy:

  • ‚Ä¢ Never access your own medical records in the system (have another employee retrieve them properly)
  • ‚Ä¢ Never access records of family, friends, or coworkers without a legitimate work reason
  • ‚Ä¢ Never look up celebrity or VIP patient records out of curiosity
  • ‚Ä¢ Never share your login credentials with anyone, including coworkers or family
  • ‚Ä¢ Never try to access systems or data outside your role
  • ‚Ä¢ Never keep access to old systems after changing roles
  • ‚Ä¢ Never use someone else's account to do your work

Consequences of Violations

Inappropriate access to patient records is a fireable offense and can result in:

  • ‚Ä¢ Immediate termination
  • ‚Ä¢ Personal HIPAA fines up to $50,000 per violation
  • ‚Ä¢ Criminal charges (up to 10 years in prison for intentional violations)
  • ‚Ä¢ Loss of professional licenses
  • ‚Ä¢ Civil lawsuits from affected patients

üîÑ When Your Role Changes

Access should change when your responsibilities change:

Promoted or Role Change

New permissions should be added. Old permissions that are no longer needed should be removed. Work with IT/manager to review and update access.

Temporary Duties

If covering for someone, request temporary access with specific end date. Don't keep it after coverage ends.

Leaving the Practice

All access should be revoked immediately on last day. Return all access badges, keys, devices.

Extended Leave

For extended leave (maternity, medical, sabbatical), access may be suspended and reactivated upon return.

  • ‚úì All access to patient records is logged and monitored
  • ‚úì Never share login credentials or use someone else's account
  • ‚úì Only access patient records you have a legitimate need to see
  • ‚úì Request new access through proper channels (manager/IT)
  • ‚úì Report suspicious access or access issues immediately
  • ‚úì When your role changes, your access should be reviewed and updated

Access control isn't about restriction - it's about protection. Limited access protects you, your coworkers, and your patients.

Endpoint Protection: Defending Your Devices

What is an Endpoint?

An endpoint is any device that connects to your network and accesses data: computers, laptops, phones, tablets, even printers. Each endpoint is a potential entry point for attackers. If one device gets infected, malware can spread to your entire network and patient data. Endpoint protection is your first line of defense.

ü¶† Threats That Target Endpoints

Malware & Viruses

Malicious software that damages systems, steals data, or allows remote control of your device

Ransomware

Encrypts your files and demands payment to unlock them. Can spread to backups and entire network

Spyware & Keyloggers

Secretly records what you type (passwords, patient data) and sends it to attackers

Trojans

Disguised as legitimate software but opens backdoors for attackers to access your system

Rootkits

Hides deep in the operating system, giving attackers persistent access while remaining invisible

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacks that exploit vulnerabilities before patches are available - advanced protection needed

Real stat: 43% of cyberattacks target small businesses, and 68% of these involve endpoint compromise. Your practice needs protection.

üõ°Ô∏è Layers of Endpoint Protection

Modern endpoint protection is multi-layered, not just antivirus:

1. Antivirus/Anti-Malware (Signature-Based)

Detects known threats by comparing files to database of virus signatures. Catches most common malware but misses new/unknown threats.

Examples: Windows Defender, Norton, McAfee, Bitdefender

2. Behavioral Analysis (Heuristics)

Watches how programs behave. If software starts encrypting files rapidly (ransomware behavior), it's blocked even if it's never been seen before.

3. Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR)

Advanced monitoring that tracks everything happening on the device. Detects sophisticated attacks, provides forensics, enables rapid response.

Examples: CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint

4. Application Control

Only allows approved applications to run. Prevents execution of malicious programs or unauthorized software.

5. Device Firewall

Controls incoming and outgoing network connections. Blocks unauthorized communication between device and internet/network.

6. Web Filtering

Blocks access to known malicious websites, phishing sites, and dangerous downloads before they reach your device.

üíª Endpoint Protection for All Device Types

Windows PCs

Built-in: Windows Defender (good baseline protection, free)

Enterprise: Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, CrowdStrike, Carbon Black, Sophos

macOS

Built-in: XProtect, Gatekeeper (Apple's built-in protection)

Third-party: Malwarebytes, Bitdefender, ESET for Mac

iOS (iPhone/iPad)

iOS is sandboxed and generally secure. Keep OS updated, don't jailbreak. Enterprise may use Mobile Device Management (MDM) for additional control.

Android

Built-in: Google Play Protect

Third-party: Bitdefender Mobile Security, Norton Mobile, Lookout. Keep OS updated, only install apps from Google Play.

Endpoint Protection: Your Role & Best Practices

‚úì How to Keep Endpoints Protected

  • ‚Ä¢ Keep antivirus running: Don't disable or uninstall security software. If it interferes with work, contact IT for proper configuration
  • ‚Ä¢ Allow scans to complete: Don't cancel scheduled scans. Let them run overnight or during breaks if needed
  • ‚Ä¢ Update security software: Antivirus definitions need daily updates to detect new threats. Enable automatic updates
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't ignore warnings: If antivirus blocks something or shows an alert, don't override it without consulting IT
  • ‚Ä¢ Keep firewall enabled: Never turn off Windows Firewall or built-in device firewalls
  • ‚Ä¢ Install software from approved sources only: Don't download random programs from the internet. Use official app stores or IT-approved sources
  • ‚Ä¢ Remove personal devices from work network: Personal laptops, tablets, or smartphones should not connect to work Wi-Fi unless approved and protected
